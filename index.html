📐 Key Grammar Rules
```

1. No Verb Tense Changes — Use Markers Instead

Pidgin verbs never conjugate. Instead, use go (future), don (past/completed), dey (present continuous).

FUT
I go chop later
I will eat later
PAST
She don go already
She has already gone
PROG
We dey wait for you
We are waiting for you

2. "Na" — The All-Purpose Emphasis Word

Na works as "is/are/it's/that's" — the most common word in Pidgin.

ID
Na me do am
It was me that did it
EQ
Na your food be this
This is your food

3. Pronouns Don't Change for Object

Same form for subject and object. I/me → I, he/him → am, they/them → dem.

EX
Tell am make e come
Tell him to come
EX
I see dem for road
I saw them on the road

4. Questions with "Wetin / How / Who"

Pidgin has its own question words: wetin (what), wey (which/that), abeg (please).

Q
Wetin you want?
What do you want?
Q
Where e dey?
Where is it?

5. "Sef" — Emphasis Particle

Sef adds emphasis, similar to "even" or "at all".

EX
I no know sef
I don't even know
EX
E no make sense sef
It doesn't make any sense at all
```
🎺 Culture & Context
```
🇳🇬

What is Nigerian Pidgin?

Nigerian Pidgin (Naijá) is an English-based creole spoken across Nigeria and parts of West Africa. It started as a trade language between English merchants and local populations in the 16th century and grew into a full creole with over 75 million speakers — Nigeria's true common tongue.

🤝

Greeting Culture

Greetings are very important in Nigerian culture. Always greet elders first. "How you dey?" (How are you?) is used constantly — the expected reply is "I dey o!" or "Fine o!" The "o" at the end adds warmth and emphasis to everything.

"How body?" → "Body dey for cloth!" (Body is in clothes = I'm doing well!)
🍛

Food Words

Chop means eat or food. "Wetin we go chop?" (What shall we eat?) is a classic Nigerian question. Food is central to social life — never turn down food at someone's home.

"Come chop!" = Come and eat! (An invitation, not just a command)
💰

Money & Hustle

Egunje (bribe), hustle (working hard to survive), mugu (a gullible person/victim) are everyday words. Nigeria's entrepreneurial spirit is reflected in the language — everyone is hustling.

"E get as e be" = It is what it is (accepting life's hardships)
🔥

Popular Slang from Music

Afrobeats artists like Burna Boy, Wizkid, and Davido have spread Pidgin globally. Words like odogwu (boss/champion), shayo (alcohol), and wahala (trouble/drama) are now heard worldwide.

"No cap, that song don mad o!" = No lie, that song is amazing!
😄

The Magic of "O"

The particle o (sometimes written oh) is attached to phrases to add emotion — surprise, warning, plea, or emphasis. It has no direct English translation but makes speech feel warm and expressive.

"E don do o!" = It's over! (with finality)
"Abeg o!" = Please! (with urgency or desperation)
```
